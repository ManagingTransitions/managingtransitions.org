Capturing Outcomes in an Emerging, Parallel Health System

Baano (Somalia) and Shaafi (Somaliland): moving from outputs to outcomes

Prepared by Matt Thomson PhD Researcher, Politics of Localisation, University of Bristol July 2026

Gap Analysis

Activity is measured. Outcomes aren't, yet.

Baano and Shaafi sit on top of the region's most reliable infrastructure: Hormuud's EVC-Plus in Mogadishu, and Telesom's ZAAD in Hargeisa. Between them, the two platforms already reach hundreds of thousands of patients across a divide that most institutions struggle to operate across at all. What neither platform yet does is measure what happens after the call ends. Both track activity, such as consults booked or prescriptions issued. Neither currently tracks whether a patient's wellbeing has improved, or the population's health has changed.

That gap matters in macro terms as much as on an individual basis. Corporate partners, Ministries of Health, and third-party stakeholders increasingly ask health practitioners to justify budgets against outcomes rather than activity, and every month this goes unmeasured is a month Baano and Shaafi under-sell what has already been built.

This note sets out a proposed design sprint: the logical framework and technical specification for an outcomes engine that converts a single IVR call or EVC-Plus consult into a longitudinal record of patient wellbeing, without forcing Somalia's and Somaliland's different systems into one shape.

Proposed Approach

Three linked parts.

01

Population-Level Outcomes Layer

+
A layer separate from either platform's existing patient management system. This is where aggregate metrics live: prevalence and general health outcomes, disaggregated by gender, age, geography, and displacement status. Built with a DHIS2-compatible export in mind, so it feeds Ministry of Health reporting rather than competing with it. Sampled properly, this layer can show value for money on specific interventions, map where conditions cluster geographically, and expose the platform's own blind spots: data collected only from people who use the service will always skew toward those with access to it, and the model needs to say so rather than hide it.
02

Patient-Level Tracking Over Time

+
Each patient needs an identifier that holds up across a shared phone and across different providers, whether that's a mobile health worker in the village or a doctor in the city, plus a way of checking in at 30, 90, and 180 days, or longer, depending on the condition. The harder design question is what counts as a good outcome when a patient also sees a traditional healer, takes black seed and honey, or simply stops calling because they've recovered. My instinct is to build a model that treats this as an ordinary part of how care is sought, rather than one that quietly records it as a platform failure.
03

The Technical Brief

+
One underlying logic with two separate exports: one built around Somalia's Baano, one around Somaliland's Shaafi. This includes a brief for a Somali-language outcome measure built from scratch, rather than a translated EQ-5D, and a proposal for using mobile money to pay small, direct compensation for completed follow-up calls, in keeping with how Transparency Solutions already compensates research participants.

TS's own Somali field staff would run every piece of community consultation and codesign, in conversation with the 4.5 clan principals and both health ministries. My role is to synthesise what comes back into system design, not to run the consultation myself. Deliverables

By the end of Phase 1, Transparency Solutions would receive:

  • An output-to-outcomes conversion matrix, covering population- and patient-level metrics
  • A dual-export technical specification, one for Somalia, one for Somaliland
  • A design brief for a locally built outcome-measuring system
  • A data governance and consent framework
  • A stakeholder engagement plan covering ministries, hospitals, distributors, and health workers

About Me

I'm not a platform developer or a Somalia specialist, that expertise already sits with Transparency Solutions. What I bring is a health-systems and localisation lens, built through several years managing that shift in practice rather than just writing about it.

At STAND, I lead multi-country partnerships across Uganda, The Gambia, Kenya, and Tanzania, including a multi-year EU co-financed programme in The Gambia. Much of that work is a smaller version of what this proposal describes: I run systems audits and focus groups for monitoring and evaluation, and the networks I manage have spent the past few years moving away from aid dependency and toward locally owned reporting, rather than metrics designed and owned externally.

Separately, through a research consortium of universities, health ministries, and NGOs, I help build academically and clinically evidenced outcome bases for prosthetics and orthotics services in low-resource settings, work that runs into the same problem Baano and Shaafi have: plenty of activity data, very little proof that people's lives actually improved. My PhD research, on how power and expertise move through the development sector, sits behind all of this rather than in front of it.

My job here is turning that field reality into a specification your engineering team can build from, not writing any of the code myself.
