Research & Analysis

Doctoral research, University of Bristol (SPAIS)

My PhD investigates the politics of localisation in Sub-Saharan Africa, focused on assistive technology (AT) and prosthetics & orthotics (P&O) networks across East and West Africa.

Rather than treating localisation as an ethical promise or a donor checklist, I examine how it functions as governance-in-practice. Grounded in critical realism and political economy, the thesis shows how aid frameworks often redistribute administrative labour, reporting burdens, and risk downward — without shifting real financial control or decision-making power.

Critical Realism Political Economy Governance-in-Practice

Crucially, the work explores how frontline actors exercise genuine agency within these limits — harnessing structural constraints through everyday material adaptations, informal workarounds, and tactical negotiation to sustain care and keep local health systems afloat.

I bridge these frameworks with field experience across The Gambia, Uganda, Kenya, and Tanzania, translating doctoral research into essays and strategic breakdowns for practitioners, evaluators, and donors — unpacking decolonial policy rhetoric, supply chain realities, and aid-dependency exits.
